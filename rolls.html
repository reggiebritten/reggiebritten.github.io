Rolls

The gamemaster will call for a roll to see if you overcome certain challenges. Each roll has a difficulty and a type, set by the GM. The difficulty of the roll is given as a dice size, with larger dice being more difficult. The type of roll will be an ability or personal value, which determine the target number (TN) for the roll. Your tools, training, expertise and especially your approach to solving the problem, will all improve the results of roll. Finally, you will roll the dice to determine how well you do.

Roll Difficulty

The GM will assign each roll a a dice size based on the task’s difficulty. The dice size is determined by considering how easy the task would be for a normal person under normal circumstances, including any strategy you are using. The dice size doesn’t depend on your proficiency at the task, the tools you use, or unusual circumstances; those are represented as bonuses or penalties to the roll. The GM may decide that some rolls are impossible unless you have the right training or tools.

Difficulty Dice Size Description Example Task
Trivial NA Everyone can do it; no roll is needed. Walking
Easy d4 Most people can do it most of the time. Lifting a table
Simple d6 Most people have even odds for success or failure. Climbing a tree
Standard d8 An untrained person will probably fail. Spotting a snake
Difficult d10 A demanding task, even for an expert. Stalking a deer
Incredible d12 Even an expert is likely to fail. Catching an arrow
Legendary d20 Even attempting it sounds ludicrous and succeeding may well be a legendary deed. Riding a dragon
Impossible NA Some things simply cannot be done… Leaping to the moon

Opposed Rolls

When another character would oppose your roll, then the GM will decide which of their abilities or personal values would pose the greatest challenge to you. That is used to set the difficulty of the roll, according to the table below. If your adversary has training that would help them oppose you, then the TN of your roll is penalized by -2. If you don’t have information about your adversary, the GM might not tell you what size dice you will roll until you commit to making the roll.

Ability / Value Dice Size
1 d4
2 d6
3 d8
4 d10
5 d12
6+ d20

Target Number

The target number, or TN, of a roll is based on the roll’s type. For example, if the GM asks for a brawn roll, and your brawn is 3, then 3 is the TN for the roll. The higher your TN is, the better your odds of success. If you have training that will help with the roll, then your training increases the roll’s TN by +2.
The tools you use can also give you a bonus to your TN. If you have a tool that is well suited for the task - beyond the bare minimum needed to attempt it - you gain a +1 bonus to your TN. If it is precisely the right tool for the job, it gives you a +2 instead. The GM might also determine that a particularly bad tool imposes a -1 penalty to your TN.

Roll Results

Roll two dice of the size set by the GM to determine the outcome of a roll. For each of the dice that comes up less than or equal to your TN, you earn one success! Count up the number of successes; each one improves the result of the roll.

  • If zero dice succeed, then the roll is a failure. Not only have you failed to accomplish your goal, but you also suffer a setback determined by the GM.

  • If your earn exactly one success, then you have achieved a mixed result. You might accomplish your goal, but suffer a setback. You might fail at your main goal, but achieve something else of value. Or you might simply make no progress and be forced to try again.

  • If at least two dice succeed, you have accomplished you goal! Things have gone as you planned.

  • If a boon gives you three or more dice, then the third successful die and each one after that earns you a fated effect! For each fated effect you earn, you may invoke a twist of fate without spending a fate point. However, the detail added to the world must be somehow related to the nature of the roll, as determined by the GM. When your enemies’ rolls earn fated effects, they will cause you to suffer a setback.

A setback resulting from a failed or mixed roll will cause a problem for you. The GM will make the consequences relevant to what you were attempting and what stakes had been set. Some more generic setbacks might involve:

  • You suffer stress, usually 1-3.

  • You suffer a condition determined by the GM.

  • You attract unwanted attention and/or generate a point of doom.

  • It takes much more time than expected, possibly leading to doom.

  • Some of your resources or equipment become lost or damaged.

  • You lose your window of opportunity.

When your goal might take more than one roll to finish, the GM will consider the total of all the dice added together. Add your ability or value, as well as any relevant training or tool, to the total of the roll. The GM will use this total to determine the amount of progress you make toward your goal, such as the amount of damage done by your attack’s yield, or how far you can move in one round. Notice that rolling lower on a die is more likely to avoid setbacks, but rolling higher will get you more progress. You can do something carefully, or do it quickly, but not both at once.

The action resolution section has more details on setbacks, mixed results, fated effects, and how you might use the total of a roll.

Boons & Banes

Boons and banes represent your character’s expertise and unusual circumstances that help or hinder a roll. The GM will also award you a boon if you come up with a very clever plan, or impose a bane if you plan has a flaw. For each boon that a roll has, roll one additional die of the same size as the rest of the dice in the roll. It counts just like the other dice for determining the success of the roll, and the roll’s total. Furthermore, if three or more dice succeed, then the roll is fated! Not only has it succeeded, you can also invoke a twist of fate for free!

Each bane that you suffer takes away one of your dice for the roll. A roll with only one bane forces you to roll one one die, and a roll with two banes takes away both your dice, making failure inevitable! Boons can add dice to rolls that have banes, canceling them out, and vice-versa.

Push Your Luck

You can choose to push your luck before a roll, giving your roll a boon, but imposing a -2 penalty to the roll’s TN. This makes failure more likely and a normal success less likely, but improves the total of your roll and makes a fated effect possible. Pushing your luck is most useful when your TN is already high. You can only push your luck once per roll, and you can’t push your luck on a yield roll.

Play it Safe

You can choose to play is safe before a roll, giving you a +2 bonus to the roll’s TN, but imposing a bane on the roll. This makes complete success impossible, but reduces the chances of total failure, likely resulting a mixed success. Playing it safe is most useful when your TN for the roll is low. You can only play it safe once per roll, and you can’t play it safe on a yield roll.

Group Rolls

Sometimes, you and your allies will make one roll that represents your success as a group. If the task is one where having multiple people is helpful, like searching a room, then you can use the highest TN of anyone in your group to determine your success. However, if having multiple people might hinder the task, like sneaking around, use the lowest TN of anyone in the group.

If any character participating in the roll has relevant tools, training, or expertise, you can add those to the highest or lowest ability or personal of anyone in the group to determine your group’s total TN and boons. As normal, multiple sources of training don’t stack, but multiple sources of expertise do.

Once you’ve determined the TN and any boons or banes, make one roll using these numbers to determine the outcome for your entire group.
