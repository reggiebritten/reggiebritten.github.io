Abilities

Talents represent a character’s innate strengths and natural capabilities. There are three talents: agility, brawn, and cunning. A typical human has a score of about one or two in each talent, but the heroes of Forge of Legends are destined for greatness. When creating a character, each player should assign seven talent points to each of the three talents in the way that best represents their character.

Each talent may be given between zero and five talent points, where zero would be considered poor and five would be extraordinary. Players should think carefully how their character’s talents would best be described. Once assigned, talents cannot be changed, although the GM may make an exception for new players.

Talents are used for talent rolls made to perform feats of skill like jumping over a gap, sneaking around, and finding hidden secrets. In combat, talents contribute to a character’s vigor (poise, momentum, and focus), used to defend against attacks and power abilities. Talents also give the character bonuses when using certain types of actions, and influence other combat attributes like speed, armor, and fated action threshold. These concepts are further explained in the Combat chapter.

Talents don’t determine how characters interact with each other or what kind of knowledge a character has acquired over the years. Those are determined by a character’s personality traits and background details, respectively.

Agility

Agility represents a character’s physical coordination and precise control of their body movements. Agile characters can gracefully elude enemies searching for them and balance, squeeze, or tumble their way into areas others would struggle to reach. In combat, agile characters excel at forcing opponents to suffer the effects of an attack and quickly finishing off worn-down foes, as well as dashing to the right place at the right time.

  • Talents rolls to move quickly, carefully, and stealthily are based on agility.

  • A character’s base poise is equal to four plus quadruple their agility. Poise is often used for abilities that require finesse and precision.

  • A character’s speed is equal to their agility. Some abilities, especially maneuvers, let a character move a number of spaces equal to their speed.

  • Whenever a character uses a weapon attack action (but not non-weapon attacks or attacks that are not actions), the threat of that attack is increased by an amount equal to the character’s agility.

  • Before a character uses any support action, they may move up to their speed.

Brawn

Brawn represents a character’s physical strength and toughness. Brawny characters can use their might to open up new paths by breaking down doors and clearing away rubble. By carrying more inventory items, a brawny character can make sure their group is always prepared and supplied. In combat, brawny characters excel at going toe-to-toe with enemies in melee combat and outlasting their opponents.

  • Talent rolls to shove heavy objects and run, swim, climb, or jump over physical obstacles are based on brawn.

  • A character’s carrying capacity, in terms of bulk, is equal to four plus double their brawn.

  • A character’s base momentum is equal to four plus quadruple their brawn. Momentum is often used for abilities that require raw brute force.

  • A character can equip armor equal to their brawn. Armor reduces the damage characters take from enemy attacks.

  • Whenever a character uses a melee attack action (but not ranged attacks or attacks that are not actions), add damage equal to your brawn to the yield of the attack.

  • After a character use a support action, they gain momentum equal to their brawn.

Insight

Cunning represents a character’s awareness, acuity, and insight. Cunning characters can get the drop on hidden enemies or traps before they can strike. Cunning character’s aren’t necessarily more intelligent, just better at noticing details and recognizing opportunities. In combat, cunning characters excel at exploiting a foe’s weakness and creating opportunities for their allies to deliver devastating attacks.

  • Talent rolls to find or notice hidden details and characters are based on your cunning.

  • A character’s perception is equal to six plus their cunning, which may help spot sneaking foes.

  • A character’s base focus is equal to four plus quadruple their cunning. Focus is often used for abilities that require careful planning and foresight.

  • A character’s fated action threshold is equal to 10 minus their cunning. A character’s actions become fated if the fate die rolls greater than or equal to their fated action threshold.

  • When a character initiates a ranged attack action (but not melee attacks or attacks that are not actions), they gain focus equal to their cunning.

  • After a character use a support action, they may give one target of the ability (possibly themself) focus equal to their cunning.

Talent Rolls

Many obstacles that characters encounter can be overcome with talent rolls. A talent roll is called for when a character attempts to do something that benefits from one of the character’s talents and where success is uncertain.

First the player should describe the activity their character will take and what they hope to accomplish with it. Next, the GM will determine how easy or difficult the activity is, and which, if any, talent applies. If the activity cannot fail, cannot succeed, or is not based on a talent, then a talent roll is unnecessary.

In addition to the character’s talent, other factors may influence the result of the talent roll. If the character has any relevant background details, each one that might help with the situation gives the character a boon on their talent roll. If there are some circumstances of the situation that make the success easier or more difficult than usual, they may also add boons or banes to the talent roll, respectively. Each boon lets the character add +2 to their talent roll, while each bane means the character subtracts -2 from the talent roll.

Legendary 18+ Even attempting it sounds ludicrous and succeeding may well be a legendary deed Riding a dragon
Impossible NA Some things simply cannot be done… Leaping to the moon

Finally, the player rolls the chance die, a 10-sided die, adds their relevant talent along with any boons and banes, and compares the result to the target number (TN) that the GM assigned. If the final result is greater than the TN, then the talent roll has succeeded, and the character has accomplished their goal. If the result is lower, then the talent roll has failed, and the character may suffer consequences as a result. The table below provides a guideline for the difficulty of a talent roll.

Alongside a chance die, the fate dice should also be rolled. The fate dice may reveal unexpected results, possibly good or bad, regardless of the success or failure of the talent roll. The fate dice, including how they are affected by boons and banes, are detailed in the Fate chapter previously.

Sometimes, it is only a matter of time before a character accomplishes their goal, such as thoroughly searching an entire room. In that case, the talent roll may represent how long it takes to succeed rather than deciding success or failure. The GM may decide how long an action takes, but the higher the roll, the faster it goes. Spending too much time can come with its own costs, outlined in the Travel & Adventuring chapter below.

Group Talent Rolls

Sometimes, multiple characters can cooperate toward resolving a talent roll. Each cooperating character can add a boon to the roll for any relevant background details from their personality. Then, only one participating character, typically the one with the highest talent, rolls for the success or failure of the whole group. The character should still consider any circumstantial boons or banes that might apply to the roll and the fate dice.

Other talent rolls may be hindered by too many participants, such as a large group trying to sneak around. In the case, players may still add together any relevant background details, but the character with the lowest total bonus will roll to determine the success or failure of the group.

Note that some circumstances may additionally raise or lower the difficulty of the roll. For example, one person trying to lift a horse might be incredible, but for four people it might only be difficult.

Opposed Talent Rolls

At times, multiple characters strive oppose each other with talent rolls. In that case each side (considering the rules for group talent rolls if applicable) makes a separate talent roll. The GM should apply boons and banes for circumstances as usual, but typically only to one side or the other. The side with the higher talent roll wins and gets the result they wanted. If both sides roll the same number, then nothing changes and things stay as they were before the roll.
