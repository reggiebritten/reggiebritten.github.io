Fate

Fate is funny thing. Heroes, especially those who stay true to themselves and to their friends, often find that destiny is on their side. In Forge of Legends, fate is a system for rewarding players for expressing their characters’ personality, and for giving players some control over the game world beyond the actions of their characters.

The most common way for characters to gain fate is through fateful deeds, momentous decisions which are described in the Personality chapter below. Characters also gain fate whenever one of their background details poses a significant obstacle to themselves or the group, as determined by the GM. Some GMs may choose to award fate points to players who make the game more fun for everyone, such as taking an amazing risk or spouting an exceptional joke.

When a player earns a fate point, they should add it to their total number and keep track of them. Each player may save up to 5 fate points at a time. If a player already has 5 fate points when they earn another, they may give their new fate point to another character instead.

Twists of Fate

Players may spend their fate points by invoking a twist of fate at any time to add detail to the game world. Some common twists of fate would be to add a feature to the environment, to add an item to their character’s inventory, or embellish their character’s background, all of which may add bonuses or penalties to certain rolls.

Any detail, no matter the subject and no matter how large or small, can be invoked by a twist of fate. However, players may only add detail, not remove or change details that have already been established.

When a player declares that they would like to invoke a twist of fate, they must describe the detail they would like to add to the world. The game master then decides how likely it would be for that detail to exist. That likelihood determines the number of fate points the player must spend to invoke the twist of fate, according to the table below. If the player agrees to spend the required fate points, then their twist of fate is manifested and the detail they suggested is added to the game world.

Twist of Fate Costs    
Description Cost Example
Likely 0 “I find a sailor down by the docks.”
Plausible 1 “I bought more rope back in town.”
Unlikely 2 “The rickety floor suddenly collapses!”
Doubtful 3 “I’ve studied this ancient, forgotten language.”
Ridiculous 5 “The duke is actually a talking rabbit.”
Impossible NA “The villain just gives up and goes home.”

Fate Dice

Alongside every talent roll, influence roll, and action ability, the fate dice will be rolled as well. Normally, only one fate die is rolled, but if the roll has boons or banes then multiple fate dice may be rolled.

Fate dice are always 10-sided dice and are never added to the talent roll, influence roll, or other roll with which they are associated. Instead, the fate dice determine whether the roll becomes fated, or whether doom befalls the players.

Whenever a player character or a friendly NPC rolls a 10 on the fate die, the roll becomes fated! When a roll is fated, a character may invoke a twist of fate costing one fewer fate point than normal. A plausible twist of fate would cost zero fate points, an unlikely twist would only cost one fate point, and so on. However, the twist of fate must be related to the roll with which it was associated. Other players or even the GM may suggest interesting twists of fate if the player cannot think of one at the moment.

Optional Rule: Rerolling Dice with Fate

A fate point can sometimes snatch victory from the jaws of defeat. If your group uses this rule, then a player may spend a point of fate to reroll a single die. It could be a talent roll, influence roll, or even one (and only one) of the fate dice themselves.

However, whenever a player or friendly NPC rolls a one on the fate die, doom befalls them. The GM will decide what form that doom takes. Sometimes, the GM will add a detail to the world that makes things more difficult for the players, just like a plausible twist of fate. Other times, the GM may add a die to the doom pool, which lingers with the players over an adventure and hinders their progress. A guide to using the doom pool can be found in the GM Guidelines chapter below.

Fate dice work slightly differently for hostile or antagonistic NPCs that it does for players or friendly NPCs. Whenever a hostile NPC rolls a 10 on a fate dice associated with a talent or influence roll, then doom befalls the players, as if one of them had rolled a 1. Whenever a hostile NPC rolls a 1, then the roll becomes fated, and one of the players may invoke a discounted twist of fate as if they had rolled a 10 on the fate die.

Note that it is possible for a roll to succeed but be doomed, or fail but become fated, just as a successful roll can be fated or a failed one doomed. However, a fated or doomed roll can’t turn success into failure or vice versa. Any details added to the world apply after the result of the roll, not during it.

In combat, an action may become fated if the fate die rolls above the character’s fated action rating, not just if it rolls 10. Fated action roll can have unique fated effects, in addition allowing a discounted twist of fate. However, if a character rolls a 1 alongside their combat action, special doomed effects may apply in combat. Remember that the fate die is only rolled alongside actions in combat, not all abilities. These rules are detailed in the Combat chapter below.

Boons and Banes

Talent rolls, influence rolls, and combat actions can all have boons or banes. In addition to adding numerical bonuses or penalties to rolls and actions, boons and banes change the way fate dice work as well.

If a roll or action has boons, then the player should roll an additional fate die for every boon. A roll with one boon rolls two fate dice, a roll with two boons rolls three fate dice, and so on. Any rolls that are fated are counted, and the roll is only doomed if all the fate dice roll 1.

For example, if a talent roll has one boon, and both fate dice roll 10, then the player may invoke a twist of fate costing two fewer fate points. If one of the dice rolls 1, and the other rolls 10, then the 1 is ignored and the player may still invoke a twist of fate costing one fewer fate point than normal.

If a roll has banes, then an additional fate die is again rolled for each bane. However, the roll is more doomed for each 1 rolled; the GM may increase the doom pool by more than one, create an even bigger setback for the players, or add two doomed effects to an action. Such a roll is only fated if all the fate dice roll high enough, not if only some do.
