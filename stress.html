Heroes will encounter many trials and tribulations in the course of their adventures. The most harrowing of these will cause characters to gain stress and eventually afflictions. Stress represents the long-term, physical, mental, and emotional burdens that a character endures, and afflictions are the palpable manifestation of that stress.

Stress

Many effects can both cause and relieve stress. The GM may assign or remove stress as seems appropriate for a given situation, but guidelines are given in the table Source of Stress & Relief.

In general, something that inflicts one stress would be described as unsettling or uncomfortable, something that inflicts three stress would be upsetting or painful, something that inflicts five stress would be, traumatic and anything that inflicts 10 or more stress would be life-threatening.

Stress tends to accumulate quickly when a character risks their life against great odds. While stress is typically relieved only by time and relaxation, both of which may be in short supply when in a hostile environment. Character’s should only expect their stress to decrease when they are surrounded by safety and civilization.

Stress does not directly impact a character’s capabilities. But accumulating too much stress can lead to serious consequences knows as afflictions.

Afflictions

Afflictions are palpable effects that impair characters who have accumulated too much stress. For every five points of stress a character has accumulated, they suffer one affliction. These afflictions take place immediately upon the character’s total stress rising to or above 5, 10, 15, 20, and 25. Whenever a character’s accumulated stress falls below one of these thresholds, the character recuperates from their oldest affliction.

The specific affliction suffered is related to the source of the stress that caused it. If the stress was caused by a physical attack or hazard, then the character will likely suffer an injury. If the source of the injury is obvious, such as a spiked floor trap causing a foot injury, the GM may assign that injury. If the nature of the injury is not obvious, such as an injury caused by an enemy’s attack, then the GM may determine an injury at random.

If the stress that caused the affliction was from some mental or emotional pain, then the GM may likewise determine which other affliction fits best. If no other affliction makes sense for the source of the stress, then the exhausted affliction may always be applied.

Some special circumstances, like a viper’s poisonous bite, can cause an affliction directly without causing any stress. Such special cases will also specify when a character recuperates from the affliction.

Whenever a character’s stress total rises above 20, they immediately suffer the unconscious condition and remain unconscious until their stress falls below 20. Once a character has suffered 30 stress, they have died, and nothing short of a miracle could bring them back.

Affliction Types

Exhausted

An exhausted character is running out of the energy needed to perform their best. A bane is added to all the character’s talent rolls, influence rolls, and actions. The character’s speed is also reduced by one A character may suffer the exhausted affliction multiple times; each adds an additional bane and further reduces their speed.

Head Injury

A character with a head injury has difficulty focusing and recalling information. All attacks against the character are unpredictable and the character adds two banes to any action that spends or gains focus. Two banes are added to the character’s cunning talent rolls, and a character with a head injury can’t contribute toward lore checks.

Chest Injury

A character with a chest injury has difficulty catching their breath and exerting themselves. Whenever the character uses an ability that costs 3 or more vigor, they suffer 1 stress. Whenever the character gains vigor, they gain 1 less.

Optional Rule: Trauma

The direst of afflictions may not always heal on their own. If an affliction is traumatic, then it isn’t removed even after the stress associated with it has been healed. A traumatic injury might be permanent damage such as a chopped off hand, while a traumatic mental affliction might represent an unresolved problem. Characters may need to see exotic healing magic or confront their inner demons once and for all to resolve their trauma. Forge of Legends is designed to be an optimistic game where nothing can keep the heroes down for long; using trauma regularly is not recommended for most groups. However, it can be used if desired or if there is a need to describe a permanent injury.

Arm Injury

A character with an arm injury has difficulty putting physical force behind any of their efforts. All attacks against the character are unblockable, and two banes are added to any action that spends or gains momentum. Two banes are also added to the character’s brawn talent rolls. A character with an arm injury doesn’t add their brawn to the amount of bulk they can carry.

Hand Injury

A character with a hand injury has difficulty holding weapons and manipulating objects. The character immediately drops whatever they are holding in one hand (chosen randomly if appropriate). The character can only wield one one-handed weapon while they have a hand injury and can’t use their empty hand for anything. Two handed weapons are not dropped but can’t be wielded. Two banes are added to any talent roll that requires the character to use their hands.

Leg Injury

A character with a leg injury has trouble coordinating their movements and moving precisely. All attacks against the character are undodgeable, and two banes are added to any action that spends or gains poise. Two banes are also added to the character’s agility talent rolls and has their speed reduced to 0.

Foot Injury

A character with a foot injury has difficulty moving quickly. Whenever the character moves, they move half the normal distance. A character with a foot injury is vulnerable to push abilities and two banes are added to any talent roll that involves moving.

Disheartened

A disheartened character is extremely disappointed with the way recent events have turned out. A disheartened character can’t gain fate and can’t invoke a twist of fate. The character suffers a bane on all their influence rolls, and the character can’t increase any of their trait ratings.

Furious

A furious is upset at the injustice of the world and intent on doing something about it soon. Two banes are added to any subtle or pragmatic influence rolls the character makes, and they can’t increase those trait ratings. A bane is also added to any ranged action the character uses.

Bitter

A bitter character has had their feelings hurt and has trouble thinking of others. Two banes are added to any idealistic or carefree influence roll the character makes, and they can’t increase those trait ratings. A bane is also added to any support action they use.

Insecure

An insecure character has had their self-confidence shaken and is hesitant to take risks. Two banes are added to any bold or serious talent rolls the character makes, and they can’t increase those trait ratings. An insecure character also has difficulty manifesting their personal legend in a way determined by the GM.

Paranoid

A paranoid character can’t trust anyone, not even their supposed “friends”. The character refuses to be targeted by support abilities and counts allied zones of control against them to determine if they are surrounded. The character is vulnerable to psychic attacks.
