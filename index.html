Basic Concepts           


After reading these basic concepts, players should understand how most of the important game rules work and be ready to start making a character. It is highly recommended for the gamemaster (often called the GM) to read through the entire book to gain a firmer grasp of the rules and better guide the players. All these rules are covered in more detail in the rest of the book.

Forge of legends uses multi-sided dice. These include 4-sided dice, called d4, 6-sided dice, called d6, and 8, 10, 12, and 20-sided dice called d8, d10, d12, and d20, respectively. Ten-sided dice (d10) are the most used, and it is recommended for players to have 3-4 such dice of different colors.

Fate               

In Forge of Legends, fate is on the side of the heroes. Fate is a resource that can be acquired and spent by players to add details to the game world. When a player uses a twist of fate to add a detail to the world, the GM will assign it a fate point cost based on how likely it is that detail exists. If the player agrees to pay that cost, their detail is added to the game world.

What the Game Isn’t

Forge of Legends shares many features in kneeling choking common with other tabletop roleplaying games. However, experienced gamers may have some expectations that don’t apply to this game in specific.

For one, characters don’t get much more powerful as they gain experience. A character’s talents almost never increase, and neither does their vigor. Instead, the majority of a character’s progression comes from gaining more options, not more power. Think of this game like a collectable card game, where you can always get more cards, but you can only use so many at a time, and none is stronger by design.

Alongside most other rolls in the game, fate dice will also be rolled. When fate dice roll high enough, players may invoke a twist of fate adding a detail to the game world costing fewer fate points. However, when the fate dice roll low, doom may befall the players.

The doom pool is a system used by the GM to structure adventures and provide meaningful setbacks. The GM can use doom in many ways, but all of them serve to put pressure on the players to complete the adventure swiftly.

Since talent rolls, influence rolls, and fate rolls all use 10-sided dice, it would be wise to roll dice of different colors, such as black or white for talent or influence rolls, and colorful dice for fate rolls.

Boons & Banes  

A wide variety of advantages, disadvantages, expertise, and circumstances can affect a character’s odds for success. Boons and banes serve to represent all those considerations.

            Any talent roll, influence roll, or attack action gains +2 for each boon associated with it. Additionally, another fate die is rolled for each boon, making fate more likely to intervene and doom more rare.

Conversely, a roll with banes subtracts -2 from those numbers. A roll with banes rolls multiple fate dice, but each can only make the roll more doomed. Boon and banes cancel each other out.


Character Creation

There are four things every character needs to begin the game: an ancestry, describing the character’s heritage, talents, describing their natural strengths and weaknesses, personality, representing how they view and interact with the world, and a class, which grants most of their abilities.

Fate Dice

Alongside every talent roll, influence roll, and action ability, the fate dice will be rolled as well. Normally, only one fate die is rolled, but if the roll has boons or banes then multiple fate dice may be rolled.

Fate dice are always 10-sided dice and are never added to the talent roll, influence roll, or other roll with which they are associated. Instead, the fate dice determine whether the roll becomes fated, or whether doom befalls the players.

Whenever a player character or a friendly NPC rolls a 10 on the fate die, the roll becomes fated! When a roll is fated, a character may invoke a twist of fate costing one fewer fate point than normal. A plausible twist of fate would cost zero fate points, an unlikely twist would only cost one fate point, and so on. However, the twist of fate must be related to the roll with which it was associated. Other players or even the GM may suggest interesting twists of fate if the player cannot think of one at the moment.

However, whenever a player or friendly NPC rolls a one on the fate die, doom befalls them. The GM will decide what form that doom takes. Sometimes, the GM will add a detail to the world that makes things more difficult for the players, just like a plausible twist of fate. Other times, the GM may add a die to the doom pool, which lingers with the players over an adventure and hinders their progress. A guide to using the doom pool can be found in the GM Guidelines chapter below.

Fate dice work slightly differently for hostile or antagonistic NPCs that it does for players or friendly NPCs. Whenever a hostile NPC rolls a 10 on a fate dice associated with a talent or influence roll, then doom befalls the players, as if one of them had rolled a 1. Whenever a hostile NPC rolls a 1, then the roll becomes fated, and one of the players may invoke a discounted twist of fate as if they had rolled a 10 on the fate die.

Note that it is possible for a roll to succeed but be doomed, or fail but become fated, just as a successful roll can be fated or a failed one doomed. However, a fated or doomed roll can’t turn success into failure or vice versa. Any details added to the world apply after the result of the roll, not during it.

In combat, an action may become fated if the fate die rolls above the character’s fated action rating, not just if it rolls 10. Fated action roll can have unique fated effects, in addition allowing a discounted twist of fate. However, if a character rolls a 1 alongside their combat action, special doomed effects may apply in combat. Remember that the fate die is only rolled alongside actions in combat, not all abilities. These rules are detailed in the Combat chapter below.

Optional Rule: Rerolling Dice with Fate

A fate point can sometimes snatch victory from the jaws of defeat. If your group uses this rule, then a player may spend a point of fate to reroll a single die. It could be a talent roll, influence roll, or even one (and only one) of the fate dice themselves.
